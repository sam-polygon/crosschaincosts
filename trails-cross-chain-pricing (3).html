Trails cross-chain interop pricing

What it costs to move stablecoins across chains into Polygon: the real network route and passthrough gas. No hidden spread, no flat-rate guessing on a market that moves daily.

Bridging has a reputation for opaque pricing because the underlying cost genuinely changes chain by chain, asset by asset, day by day. Trails abstracts that complexity into one predictable formula — pass-through cost plus a fixed 3 bps — and picks the cheapest, fastest verified route automatically. What's below is what a transfer actually costs, not a marked-up estimate.

Pricing model

The model, in three rules

01
Price = route cost + gas + Polygon's bps to manage.

The underlying network route and gas pass straight through at cost — we don't mark up a moving rate. Polygon charges a flat 3 bps on every transfer to manage that variability: the route cost and gas move with the market, the 3 bps margin does not.

02
Two speed modes, same destination.

A fast path settles in seconds — the standard for consumer-facing transfers, where confirmation needs to feel instant. A slower path settles in minutes and is typically free at the network level, suited to larger, less time-sensitive volume.

03
Large transfers route through the deep-liquidity path by default.

Transfers above $50K default to the slow/free path, where liquidity is deepest and slippage is not a factor. There's no size limit on this path.

On $1,000 transfers, cost is a gas story everywhere except Tron, where fixed network gas is roughly 90% of the total. Below ~$1,000 per transfer, gas dominates further — a $100 Tron-origin transfer runs 200–300 bps in gas alone.

Gas is passed through at cost

Most chains — Polygon, L2s, Avalanche — cost $0.01–0.05 per transfer, invisible on anything above $1,000. Ethereum runs closer to $0.10 and spikes with congestion. Tron is the outlier at $2–3 per transfer, driven by its fixed fee structure rather than anything Trails controls.

Where the free tier is fast

The free/slow tier isn't always slow. On fast-finality origin chains — Polygon, Avalanche, BNB Chain, and a growing list of newer networks — the free tier settles in seconds, not minutes. Speed premiums only exist where the source chain itself has slow finality.

Coverage

Built for breadth, not just the majors

Beyond the core USDC/USDT corridors, Trails routes stablecoins and other majors (ETH, WBTC, and a growing long tail) across 20+ networks — including Unichain, HyperEVM, Monad, Sei, Linea, Starknet, and more coming online regularly. Every route runs through the same pricing model: underlying cost, passed through.

Newer or lower-volume routes are priced dynamically at the time of transfer, since they typically ride intent-based liquidity networks rather than a fixed canonical rail.

Roadmap

What brings these costs down further

Three levers bring the numbers above down further:

→
Polygon Solver — our in-house execution layer.

Reduced reliance on third-party liquidity networks where it's the better path. Trails always routes each transfer to whichever option is fastest and cheapest at that moment — so the Solver isn't the default, it's one more competing path, and it wins the route when it's genuinely the best price and speed, including at scale.

→
Batching.

Bundling many transfers into a single onchain transaction, so fixed gas is split across all of them instead of charged per transfer. Biggest impact on higher-gas chains and small-ticket flows everywhere.

→
A dedicated liquidity pool for branded stablecoins.

For partners who issue their own stablecoin, a shared liquidity pool converts it 1:1 with USDC/USDT — no exchange spread, no market fees.
